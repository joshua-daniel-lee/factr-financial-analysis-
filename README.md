## Business Model Summary

**In Plain English:** We make money three ways—subscriptions (our main income), referral fees when users upgrade, and unused credits. We pay publishers fairly for content while keeping 35% to cover our costs and grow the business.

### Revenue Streams (3 Sources)
1. **Subscription Revenue** (81% of total)
   - Lite: $15/month for 30 credits → 70% of users
   - Pro: $40/month for 100 credits → 25% of users  
   - Enterprise: $100/user/month → 5% of users

2. **Affiliate Fees** (12% of total)
   - 15% commission when users convert to direct publisher subscriptions
   - Aligns incentives with publishers (we're a funnel, not a competitor)

3. **Credit Breakage** (7% of total)
   - 8% of purchased credits go unused (standard Software as a Service (SaaS)/marketplace dynamic)

### Cost Structure
- **Publisher Payouts:** 65% of recognized credit revenue ($0.50/article avg)
- **Operating Expenses:** Scaling from $3.9M (Year 1) to $13.7M (Year 5)
- **Customer Acquisition:** $75→$40 declining CAC as organic growth kicks in

## Financial Terms Glossary

### Revenue & Growth Metrics

**Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR)**: The predictable revenue a company expects to receive annually from subscriptions. For SaaS businesses, this is the value of all active subscription contracts normalized to a year.

**Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR)**: The rate at which revenue grows each year on average over a specific period. A 58% CAGR means revenue grows by 58% per year on average.

**Churn**: The rate at which customers cancel their subscriptions. Lower churn means better customer retention. Monthly churn of 2% means 2% of customers leave each month.

**Cohort Analysis**: Tracking groups of customers who signed up in the same time period to see how their behavior changes over time.

### Profitability & Unit Economics Metrics

**Lifetime Value (LTV)**: The total revenue expected from a customer over their entire relationship with the company. Calculated by multiplying average revenue per month by customer lifetime.

**Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**: The total cost of sales and marketing needed to acquire one new customer. Includes advertising, sales team salaries, and marketing expenses divided by number of customers acquired.

**LTV:CAC Ratio**: The relationship between customer lifetime value and acquisition cost. A 11.5x ratio means for every $1 spent acquiring a customer, the company earns $11.50 back. Above 3x is considered healthy.

**CAC Payback Period**: The time it takes to earn back the money spent acquiring a customer. A 5-month payback means the company recoups its acquisition costs in 5 months of customer revenue.

**Gross Margin**: Revenue minus cost of goods sold, expressed as a percentage. A 35% gross margin means the company keeps $0.35 of every dollar after paying for what it sells.

**Contribution Margin**: Revenue minus variable costs (costs that change with each sale). Shows profitability at the unit level before fixed costs.

**EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization)**: A measure of operating profitability that shows earnings before accounting for financing costs, taxes, and non-cash expenses.

### Investment & Return Metrics

**Internal Rate of Return (IRR)**: The annual rate of return an investment generates over time. An 18.4% IRR means investors earn 18.4% per year on their investment.

**Net Present Value (NPV)**: The current value of future cash flows, discounted back to today's dollars. Accounts for the time value of money (a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow).

**Return on Investment (ROI)**: The profit from an investment relative to its cost. A 17x ROI means for every $1 invested, you get $17 back.

**Exit Multiple**: How many times your investment you receive when the company is sold. A 15x multiple means if you invested $1, you receive $15 at exit.

**Terminal Value**: The estimated value of the company beyond the forecast period (after Year 5), used in NPV calculations.

**Discount Rate**: The rate used to convert future cash flows to present value. Typically 10% for venture capital, reflecting the risk and expected return.

### Business Model Terms

**Software as a Service (SaaS)**: A business model where software is delivered over the internet via subscription rather than installed locally.

**Business-to-Business (B2B)**: Selling products or services to other companies rather than individual consumers.

**Business-to-Business-to-Consumer (B2B2C)**: A model where a company sells to businesses who then provide the service to their employees or customers.

**Break-Even**: The point where total revenue equals total costs, meaning the company is no longer losing money.

**Burn Rate**: The rate at which a company spends cash, typically measured monthly. Shows how quickly the company is using its funding.

### Capital & Financial Structure

**Capital Expenditures (CapEx)**: Money spent to acquire or upgrade physical assets like equipment, technology, or buildings. One-time investments depreciated over time.

**Operating Expenses (OpEx)**: Ongoing costs to run the business like salaries, rent, and utilities. These are regular, recurring expenses.

**Seed Round**: The first official round of venture capital funding, typically $1-5M, used to build the initial product and prove the business model.

**Series A**: The second major funding round, typically $5-15M, used to scale user acquisition and operations after proving product-market fit.

**Series B**: The third major funding round, typically $10-30M+, used to scale aggressively and reach profitability.

**Dilution**: The reduction in ownership percentage when new investors buy shares. 20% dilution means founders go from owning 100% to 80%.

### Technology & Operations

**Machine Learning/Artificial Intelligence (ML/AI)**: Technologies that enable computers to learn from data and make intelligent decisions without explicit programming.

**Federated Credential Management (FedCM)**: A browser technology that allows users to sign in to websites using identity providers in a privacy-preserving way.

**Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: A metric measuring customer satisfaction and loyalty. Scores above 50 are excellent. Calculated by asking "How likely are you to recommend us?"

**Initial Public Offering (IPO)**: When a private company first sells shares to the public on a stock exchange.

**Private Equity (PE)**: Investment firms that buy companies using a combination of debt and equity, often to restructure and resell them.

**Letters of Intent (LOIs)**: Non-binding documents expressing serious interest in a partnership or transaction.

**Comma-Separated Values (CSV)**: A file format for storing tabular data in plain text, where each line is a data record and fields are separated by commas.

**Venture Capital (VC)**: Investment firms that provide funding to startups and early-stage companies with high growth potential in exchange for equity.

**Profit & Loss (P&L)**: A financial statement showing revenues, costs, and expenses over a period. Also called an income statement.
